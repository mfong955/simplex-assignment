## Task 1 — Experimental Setup

### Generative Process

I train a small transformer via next-token prediction on a **non-ergodic** dataset built from K=3 Mess3 hidden Markov models.  Each sequence in the training corpus is generated by exactly one randomly-chosen source; the joint process over sequences has no single stationary distribution.

**The three Mess3 sources** are parameterised by (a, x) where a controls diagonal dominance (autocorrelation) and x controls cross-emission probability:

| Source | a    | x    | Character |
|--------|------|------|-----------|
| 0      | 0.95 | 0.05 | Highly structured — stays in state, tokens very predictable |
| 1      | 0.60 | 0.15 | Moderate structure (Shai2026 parameters) |
| 2      | 0.30 | 0.25 | Noisy — rapid state mixing, tokens near-uniform |

**Mess3 transition tensor** (following fwh_core convention):

```
T[obs, state_from, state_to] = P(emit obs AND go to state_to | in state_from)
```

Each column of T_net = ∑_obs T[obs] sums to 1 (column-stochastic in state dimension).  For all three parameter choices the stationary distribution is uniform: π = [⅓, ⅓, ⅓] by the three-fold symmetry of Mess3.

**Belief update** at each observed token x_t:

```
b_t(s) ∝  (b_{t-1} @ T[x_t])[s]    (row-vector left-multiply then normalize)
```

**Vocabulary** {0, 1, 2, BOS=3}, vocab_size = 4.  BOS is prepended to every sequence; the model never predicts BOS.

### Model

A two-layer causal transformer (TransformerLens `HookedTransformer`):

| Hyperparameter | Value |
|---|---|
| d_model | 128 |
| n_heads / d_head | 4 / 32 |
| n_layers | 2 |
| d_mlp | 512 |
| n_ctx | 64 |
| seq_len (training) | 48 tokens + BOS |
| Activation | GELU + LayerNorm |

Total parameters: ~440k.  Small enough to train in <30 min on a single GPU.

### Training

- **Loss:** cross-entropy next-token prediction on tokens positions 1..T+1 (predict each observation from its context)
- **Optimiser:** AdamW, lr=1e-3, weight_decay=1e-4, β=(0.9, 0.95)
- **Schedule:** linear warmup (500 steps) → cosine decay to 10% lr
- **Steps:** 10,000 × batch_size=256 ≈ 2.56M token pairs

## Task 2 — Pre-registered Geometric Prediction

*Written before any training run.*

### Bayesian belief state structure

The optimal predictor for this process must maintain an internal state that is sufficient to compute the next-token distribution.  That sufficient statistic is the **joint belief vector**:

$$\mathbf{b}_t = \bigl\{ w_k(t),\; \mathbf{h}_k(t) \bigr\}_{k=0}^{K-1}$$

where

$$w_k(t) = P(\text{source}=k \mid x_{1:t}), \qquad \mathbf{h}_k(t) = P(\text{hidden state} \mid x_{1:t},\; \text{source}=k) \in \Delta^2.$$

**Dimensionality:** The joint belief has $3K - 1 = 8$ free parameters (K-1=2 source weights are free, plus 2 per hidden simplex × K=3 sources).  The belief state lives in $\Delta^{3K-1} = \Delta^8$ (the 8-dimensional face of a 9-simplex).

Note: unlike the paper's setting where K parallel factors combine (saving exponential dimensions), here K sources in a non-ergodic mixture have the *same* total dimension as the joint representation.  The question is therefore entirely about **geometry**, not dimension count.

### Source-weight dynamics

The source weights evolve as a Bayesian update driven by the sequence likelihood ratio:

$$w_k(t) = \frac{w_k(0) \prod_{\tau=1}^t p_k(x_\tau \mid x_{1:\tau-1})}{\sum_j w_j(0) \prod_{\tau=1}^t p_j(x_\tau \mid x_{1:\tau-1})}$$

Under reasonable distinguishability conditions the dominant source concentrates: $w_k(t) \to \mathbf{e}_k$ as $t \to \infty$.  The **rate of concentration** depends on the KL divergence between sources' next-token distributions; Source 0 (very structured) is identified fastest, Source 2 (noisy) slowest.

### Geometric prediction (pre-registered)

**Prediction 1 — Cluster formation.**  At final context positions the residual stream should form K ≈ 3 separated clusters in PCA space, one per source.  Each cluster should lie approximately on a 2-dimensional manifold (a distorted image of the Mess3 belief simplex Δ^2 for that source).

**Prediction 2 — Effective dimensionality decreases with context.**  At position t=0 (after BOS, before any observations) the transformer has no information about which source is active; the full 8-dimensional joint belief is relevant.  As t increases and $w_k(t)$ concentrates onto one source, the predictive state collapses toward a single 2D simplex.  Concretely:

$$\text{eff-rank}(t=0) \approx 8, \qquad \text{eff-rank}(t \to \infty) \approx 2.$$

This is a novel, testable prediction: **effective dimensionality is a monotonically decreasing function of context position.**  The rate of decrease should track source entropy $H[k \mid x_{1:t}]$.

**Prediction 3 — Factored subspace structure (FWH).**  If the transformer internalises the factored structure of the generative process, the residual stream should organise into (K+1) approximately orthogonal subspaces:

- 1 × "source subspace" spanned by the 2 dimensions encoding $w_k(t)$ (a copy of $\Delta^2$)
- K × "hidden subspaces", one per source, each encoding $\mathbf{h}_k(t)$ (another copy of $\Delta^2$)

Expected subspace overlaps: **diagonal ~1, off-diagonal ~0.**  This is the FWH prediction applied to the non-ergodic mixture.

**Prediction 4 — Source decodability precedes hidden decodability.**  At early context positions, $w_k(t)$ is more uncertain (nearing uniform) but the per-source hidden beliefs $\mathbf{h}_k(t)$ are already well-defined.  The linear R² for source weights should start low and rise to ~1 quickly; hidden belief R² should be high even at early positions for dominant-source sequences.

### Geometric intuition

Think of the full belief space as a **product of simplices**: $\Delta^{K-1} \times (\Delta^2)^K$.  The source weight component lives in a triangle; each hidden belief component lives in its own triangle.  The FWH bias predicts the transformer will embed each component in a separate, roughly orthogonal 2D planar subspace of $\mathbb{R}^{128}$, yielding a 2+2+2+2=8 dimensional representation with near-zero cross-subspace covariance.

The geometric evolution with context position is like watching a tensor product collapse: the source weight triangle "sharpens" toward a vertex (one source wins), which decouples the hidden state triangles for the losing sources from the residual stream.

## Task 3 — Post-training Analysis

*(Results filled in after training.)*

### 3a. Cumulative Explained Variance (CEV)

We compute PCA on all residual stream activations pooled across all context positions and all sequences.  The **effective rank** (number of components to explain 95% variance) is reported.

Expected: effective rank ≈ 8 globally (but see context-position analysis in Task 4 for finer structure).

See: `results/figures/fig3_cev_curve.png`

### 3b. Linear decodability of belief states

We fit a cross-validated Ridge regression from residual stream activations (d=128) to:
- Joint belief vector (3K=9 dims, but 8 free)
- Source weights (K=3 dims)
- Hidden beliefs, all sources (K×3=9 dims)

**Metric:** cross-validated R² (5-fold).  A high R² means the belief state is linearly readable from the residual stream; the model has not compressed this information into a nonlinear code.

See: `results/figures/fig4_belief_regression.png`, `fig5_per_dim_r2.png`

### 3c. Subspace overlap

We identify the following factor subspaces via Ridge regression + SVD (taking the top-2 right singular vectors of each coefficient matrix):
- Source subspace (captures variance in source weights)
- Hidden subspace k, for k=0,1,2 (captures variance in hidden beliefs of source k, using only sequences where source k is dominant)

Pairwise overlap: $\text{overlap}(A,B) = \|\sigma\|^2 / \min(\dim A, \dim B)$ where $\sigma$ are the singular values of $A^\top B$ (both bases orthonormalised).

**FWH prediction:** diagonal block = 1, off-diagonal ≈ 0.

See: `results/figures/fig6_subspace_overlap.png`

## Task 4 — Context-Position-Dependent Geometry

I chose the **context-position-dependent geometry** analysis because it directly tests the dynamic aspect of belief inference: how the transformer's internal representation evolves as evidence accumulates within a context window.

### Motivation

In-context learning (ICL) is well-described as implicit Bayesian inference over latent variables (the source identity here).  The residual stream at position t should encode the posterior $P(\text{source}, \text{hidden state} \mid x_{1:t})$.  The **geometry** of this posterior changes qualitatively with t:

- **Early t:** high source uncertainty → full 8D joint belief relevant
- **Late t:** source resolved → 2D residual (one Mess3 triangle for the winning source)

This is directly relevant to Simplex's goal of understanding how transformers represent belief states during ICL.

### Analysis

For each context position t ∈ {0, 1, ..., 48}:

1. **Effective rank:** PCA on activations at that position across all N sequences → rank-at-95%-CEV
2. **Source decodability:** linear R² from acts[:, t, :] → source_weights[:, t, :]
3. **Hidden decodability:** weighted linear R² from acts[:, t, :] → hidden_beliefs[:, t, k, :] (weighted by w_k)
4. **Source entropy:** mean H[k | x_{1:t}] over all sequences

### Interpretation

If predictions hold, this constitutes direct evidence that:
1. Transformers track Bayesian posterior belief states in their residual stream
2. The geometry of the residual stream is context-dependent and mirrors the information-theoretic collapse of uncertainty
3. The FWH bias persists even in the non-ergodic setting, organising belief factors into orthogonal subspaces

If the effective rank *does not* decrease, it would suggest the model is not tracking source identity efficiently — possibly encoding it in a distributed rather than factored way.

## Methodological notes

**Why not use the factored-reps infrastructure?**  The existing Hydra/MLflow setup is designed for the paper's joint-factor setting (K independent parallel processes).  For the non-ergodic mixture the data format, belief computation, and analysis questions are different enough that a clean, self-contained implementation is clearer and more portable for review.  The `mess3()` matrix formula and the HookedTransformer usage are taken directly from the repo.

**Cross-validation for regression:**  We use 5-fold CV with Ridge regularisation (α=1e-3) rather than in-sample R² to avoid overfitting to the high-dimensional residual stream.  The R² target is the population of *ground-truth* belief states, which have 8 meaningful dimensions in 128-dimensional space.

**Subspace identification:**  We use the right singular vectors of Ridge regression coefficients rather than PCA on residuals, because regression coefficients explicitly identify the subspace *covariant* with the factor, not just the subspace of maximal variance (which could be dominated by other factors).
