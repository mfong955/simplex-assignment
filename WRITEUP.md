## Task 1 — Experimental Setup

### Generative Process

I train a small transformer via next-token prediction on a **non-ergodic** dataset
built from K=3 Mess3 hidden Markov models. Each sequence in the training corpus is
generated by exactly one randomly-chosen source; the joint process over sequences
has no single stationary distribution.

The three Mess3 sources are parameterised by $(\alpha, x)$ where $\alpha$ controls
diagonal dominance (autocorrelation) and $x$ controls cross-emission probability:

| Source | $\alpha$ | $x$ | Character |
|--------|----------|-----|-----------|
| 0 | 0.95 | 0.05 | Highly structured — stays in state, tokens very predictable |
| 1 | 0.60 | 0.15 | Moderate structure |
| 2 | 0.30 | 0.25 | Noisy — rapid state mixing, tokens near-uniform |

Vocabulary $\{0, 1, 2, \text{BOS}=3\}$, vocab size 4. BOS is prepended to every
sequence; the model never predicts BOS.

### Model and Training

A two-layer causal transformer (TransformerLens `HookedTransformer`), $d_\text{model}=128$,
4 heads, $d_\text{mlp}=512$, context length 17 (~440k parameters). Trained with
AdamW (lr=1e-3, weight\_decay=1e-4), linear warmup then cosine decay, 10k steps at
batch size 256.

### Why is this structure interesting for language models?

Real language is not ergodic. A sequence from a scientific journal and a sequence
from a social media post have different transition probabilities — different
generative processes — and the model must track which one is active rather than
averaging over all of them. The ensemble average is not the time average.

The non-ergodic Mess3 setup is a minimal, fully controlled analogue of this domain
and register structure: multiple distinct sources, each internally consistent, never
mixed within a sequence. It is a clean test of whether transformers can perform
**hierarchical Bayesian inference** — inferring not just "what hidden state is the
generator in" but "which generator is active." In natural language we cannot compute
the ground-truth posterior over sources, but in this synthetic setting we can. This
lets us ask precisely: does the model's residual stream track the correct Bayesian
posterior, and does it do so in the orthogonal factored geometry the paper predicts?

If the factored world hypothesis extends to real LLMs — which the paper's inductive
bias argument suggests it should — then the factors the model discovers may
correspond to interpretable, separable aspects of language: topic, syntactic
register, factual domain, or whether a claim is hedged or confident. If those
factors live in orthogonal subspaces of the residual stream, they become readable
and adjustable independently. The non-ergodic Mess3 experiment is the controlled
proof-of-concept that makes that larger goal tractable to reason about formally.

### Other experiments with more time

The most direct extension would be applying CEV analysis to a large pretrained
LLM's residual stream with no architectural modifications. Feed the model a large
corpus of diverse text passages drawn from distinct domains (legal, scientific,
literary, conversational), collect residual stream activations at each token
position, and run PCA. Is effective dimensionality consistent with factored
structure? Do passages from different domains cluster into distinct geometric
regions? A complementary experiment would adapt the vary-one procedure to natural
language using minimal pairs — text pairs differing in exactly one property (e.g.
grammatical number, tense, topic) — to isolate candidate factor subspaces and test
their pairwise orthogonality using the overlap metric from the paper.

## Task 2 — Pre-Registered Geometric Prediction

*All predictions written before any training run or residual stream analysis.*

### 2.1 What Makes This Setup Different From the Paper

The paper's main experiments study processes that are independent or conditionally
independent, where multiple factors run in parallel and the model tracks belief over
all of them simultaneously. This setup introduces a qualitatively different
structure: **non-ergodicity**. Each training sequence is generated by exactly one
of three Mess3 sources, fixed for its entire length, and the joint process over
sequences has no single stationary distribution — the ensemble average is not the
time average.

This means the model must perform **hierarchical inference**: simultaneously
inferring which source is active and tracking the internal hidden state of that
source. These are two nested levels of uncertainty that the paper's framework did
not directly study.

---

### 2.2 Structure of the Optimal Belief State

The sufficient statistic for optimal next-token prediction is the joint belief
vector:

$$\mathbf{b}_t = \bigl\{ w_k(t),\; \mathbf{h}_k(t) \bigr\}_{k=0}^{K-1}$$

where $w_k(t) = P(\text{source}=k \mid x_{1:t})$ and
$\mathbf{h}_k(t) = P(\text{hidden state} \mid x_{1:t}, \text{source}=k) \in \Delta^2$.

The source weights live in a 2-simplex $\Delta^2$. Each per-source hidden belief
also lives in a 2-simplex $\Delta^2$ (3 Mess3 hidden states). Together, the full
belief covers $3K=9$ combinations constrained to sum to 1:

$$\mathbf{b}_t \in \Delta^8 \subset \mathbb{R}^9, \qquad \dim = 3K - 1 = 8$$

breaking down as $K-1=2$ free source weights plus $2K=6$ free hidden state
coordinates.

An important contrast with the paper: their $K$ parallel independent factors yield
a factored representation exponentially cheaper than the joint one. Here, $K$
non-ergodic sources have the *same* total dimensionality as the joint representation
— 8 dimensions either way. The question is therefore not about dimensional savings
but entirely about **geometric structure and organization**.

---

### 2.3 Geometric Predictions

#### Geometry at long context

At late context positions, the residual stream should organize into $K=3$ separated
clusters in PCA space, one per source. Within each cluster, activations should lie
on a 2-dimensional manifold — a distorted image of the Mess3 belief simplex
$\Delta^2$ for that source, consistent with Figure 2a of the paper.

If the transformer internalizes the factored structure, this cluster structure is
the visible consequence of a deeper organization: the residual stream partitions
into 4 approximately orthogonal subspaces according to the Factored World
Hypothesis:

- **1 source subspace** (2D): encodes $w_k(t)$. The three clusters in PCA space
  are the three corners of this subspace's $\Delta^2$.
- **3 hidden subspaces** (2D each): one per source, each encoding $\mathbf{h}_k(t)$
  as a copy of that source's Mess3 belief simplex.

Total predicted dimensions: $2 + 3 \times 2 = 8$. Pairwise subspace overlap should
be near zero — an overlap matrix with diagonal $\approx 1$, off-diagonal $\approx 0$.

Source 0 ($\alpha=0.95$) is most statistically distinctive, so its cluster should
be most cleanly separated. Sources 1 and 2 should be closer, particularly at short
context.

#### How geometry changes with context position

At $t=0$ (BOS only), the model has no source evidence; the full 8-dimensional joint
belief is relevant. As $w_k(t)$ concentrates onto one source, the predictive state
collapses toward a single 2D simplex. Predicted effective dimensionality:

$$\text{eff-rank}(t=0) \approx 8, \qquad \text{eff-rank}(t \to \infty) \approx 2$$

This gives a novel testable prediction: **effective dimensionality is a
monotonically decreasing function of context position**, with rate of decrease
tracking source entropy $H[k \mid x_{1:t}]$. Disambiguation rate is governed by KL
divergence between sources' next-token distributions, so Source 0 should be
identified fastest.

#### How geometry changes across layers

Following the paper's general finding, geometric structure should be most
pronounced at final layers: high effective dimensionality after the embedding,
emerging cluster separation after layer 1, and cleanest factored geometry after
layer 2.

---

### 2.4 Alternative Geometries

**Joint representation.** Single 8D simplex over all (source, hidden state)
combinations, no sub-clustering, no orthogonal organization.

**Source-only representation.** Three separated clusters but no Mess3 simplex
geometry within each — the model tracks source identity but not within-source
hidden state. Plausible given the absence of compression pressure.

**Collapsed ergodic representation.** A single cloud with blended Mess3 geometry
and no source clustering — the model fails to discover the non-ergodic structure
entirely.

The full factored structure (FWH) is the pre-registered prediction based on the
inductive bias argument in the paper. Source-only is considered the most plausible
alternative.

---

### 2.5 Geometric Intuition

The full belief space is a product of simplices: one source-weight triangle and
three hidden-belief triangles. The FWH predicts the transformer embeds each in a
separate, roughly orthogonal 2D subspace of $\mathbb{R}^{128}$ — four orthogonal
triangles, each independently tracking one level of the hierarchy.

Geometrically, the evolution with context is a progressive collapse: the source
weight triangle sharpens toward a corner as evidence accumulates, the losing
sources' hidden triangles become irrelevant, and the full 8D structure contracts to
an effectively 2D structure at sequence end.

## Task 3 — Residual Stream Geometry Analysis

### 3.1 Global Structure at Final Context Position

![Residual stream PCA at final token position](figures/fig1_pca.png)

After training to a final cross-entropy loss of 0.9438 nats (theoretical optimum
~0.89 nats), PCA of residual stream activations at the final token position reveals
that the first 3 principal components explain 96.7% of total variance — effective
rank 3, substantially lower than the predicted 8 dimensions for either the joint or
factored representation. The model has found a more compressed solution than either
hypothesis anticipated.

The dominant structure is a clean separation of Source 0 ($\alpha=0.95$) from
Sources 1 and 2 along PC1, which alone accounts for 83.8% of variance. Sources 1
and 2 overlap heavily. This asymmetry is expected: Source 0 has dramatically lower
token entropy than Sources 1 and 2 (which differ by only ~0.05 nats in entropy
rate), making it far more statistically distinctive within a 16-token context
window.

![3D PCA and 2D projections](figures/fig1b_pca_3d.png)

PC1 functions as a source-identity axis separating Source 0 from the rest. PC2 and
PC3 capture within-cluster variation: Source 0's cluster is visibly elongated along
these axes, consistent with Mess3 belief simplex geometry. The overall geometry is
better described as one dominant source-identity axis plus lower-dimensional
within-cluster variation, rather than the four orthogonal subspaces predicted by
the FWH.

---

### 3.2 How Geometry Evolves With Context Position

![Effective rank by token position](figures/fig2_eff_rank_by_position.png)

Tracking effective rank across token positions reveals a non-monotonic trajectory
not anticipated in the pre-registered predictions:

- **Position 0 (BOS):** Rank = 1. All sequences are identical at BOS — no variance.
- **Positions 1–7:** Rank rises to 4 as the model accumulates evidence about source
  identity and hidden state simultaneously. This is the source-disambiguation phase.
- **Positions 8–15:** Rank drops to 3 and stabilizes. Source identity has been
  sufficiently resolved, and the representation collapses onto the identified
  source's belief simplex.

The trajectory 1 → 4 → 3 tells a coherent story: the model expands its
representational dimensionality to perform source inference, then contracts once
inference has converged. The final rank of 3 (rather than 2) likely reflects
residual uncertainty between Sources 1 and 2, which remain difficult to
disambiguate within 16 tokens.

---

### 3.3 Linear Decodability of Belief Components

![Linear decodability at final position](figures/fig3_decodability.png)

Linear regression from residual stream activations to ground-truth belief states
reveals a striking asymmetry between the two levels of the hierarchy:

**Hidden state beliefs are almost perfectly linearly decodable.** Cross-validated
$R^2$ values at the final position are 0.97 (Source 0), 0.83 (Source 1), and 0.95
(Source 2) — despite the model never receiving explicit hidden-state supervision.
The within-source Mess3 belief geometry is linearly accessible from the residual
stream, consistent with the FWH prediction.

**Source weight beliefs are poorly linearly decodable** ($R^2 = 0.37$). This is not
because the information is absent — PCA shows clear cluster separation for Source 0
— but because source identity appears to be encoded as categorical cluster
membership rather than as a continuous linear direction. A nonlinear classifier
would likely achieve high accuracy.

This asymmetry has a natural explanation grounded in the training objective. The
hidden state directly determines the next-token distribution, so the model has
strong gradient pressure to encode it linearly and precisely. Source identity is a
higher-level latent variable that influences predictions only indirectly, through
its effect on which hidden state dynamics are operating. The model uses source
identity for clustering but has no gradient pressure to maintain it as a continuous
linear quantity.

---

### 3.4 Comparison to Pre-Registered Predictions

| Prediction | Result | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| 3 separated clusters, each a 2D Mess3 simplex | Source 0 separates cleanly; Sources 1–2 overlap. Source 0 shows elongated simplex geometry. | Partially confirmed |
| Eff. rank decreases monotonically 8 → 2 | Rank follows 1 → 4 → 3, non-monotonic. Starting rank 1, final rank 3. | Not confirmed — but terminal decrease and final value are in the right spirit |
| 4 orthogonal subspaces (1 source + 3 hidden) | One dominant source-identity axis, not 4 orthogonal subspaces. | Not confirmed |
| Hidden $R^2$ high; source $R^2$ starts low and rises | Hidden $R^2$ = 0.83–0.97. Source $R^2$ rises only to 0.37. | Confirmed in direction; source $R^2$ never reaches expected values |

The most significant departure is in the effective rank trajectory. The 8 → 2
monotonic decrease assumed the model would represent full source uncertainty at
sequence start. Instead the model found a more compressed strategy: 4 dimensions
during active disambiguation, collapsing to 3 once the dominant source is
identified, never requiring the full 8D joint belief space. This likely reflects the
inductive bias toward dimensionally efficient representations documented in the
paper — the model found a solution cheaper than the full joint representation even
though no compression savings were theoretically guaranteed in this setting.

The hidden belief decodability results are the clearest success of the theoretical
framework. Even in a non-ergodic setting where the dimensional savings argument does
not apply, the model precisely tracks within-source Mess3 belief geometries in
linearly accessible subspaces — consistent with the paper's core claim that
next-token prediction induces geometrically organized belief representations.
However, the failure to linearly encode source identity suggests the inductive bias
toward factored linear representations is weaker for higher-level latent variables
that influence predictions only indirectly.

## Task 4 — Geometry Under Varying Source Distinguishability

### 4.1 Motivation

Task 3 revealed two related findings that invite systematic follow-up. First, source
identity was poorly linearly decodable (R² = 0.37) despite clear cluster separation
in PCA space, suggesting categorical rather than continuous encoding. Second,
Sources 1 and 2 failed to separate cleanly, likely because their entropy rates are
too similar to disambiguate within 16 tokens. Both findings point to the same
underlying variable: **source distinguishability**.

This experiment varies distinguishability directly by fixing two anchor sources at
the extremes and sweeping a third through the parameter space between them. The
goal is to identify when FWH predictions succeed or fail, and whether the encoding
of source identity transitions from categorical to linear as sources become more
distinct.

---

### 4.2 Experimental Design

Two sources are fixed as anchors:

| Source | $\alpha$ | $x$ | Role |
|--------|----------|-----|------|
| Anchor A | 0.95 | 0.05 | Highly structured, low entropy |
| Anchor B | 0.30 | 0.25 | Noisy, high entropy |

A probe source sweeps between them via a mixing parameter $\lambda \in [0, 1]$:

$$\alpha(\lambda) = 0.95 - 0.65\lambda, \qquad x(\lambda) = 0.05 + 0.20\lambda$$

At $\lambda=0$ the probe matches Anchor A; at $\lambda=1$ it matches Anchor B.
Suggested sweep: $\lambda \in \{0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 1.0\}$, with a
separate transformer trained from scratch for each condition. Sweeping $\lambda$
continuously tunes the KL divergence between the probe and both anchors — which
theory predicts is the primary driver of disambiguation speed and representational
geometry.

---

### 4.3 What We Would Measure

For each $\lambda$, the same analysis pipeline as Tasks 2–3:

**Geometry:** PCA cluster separation at final position; effective rank at final
position and across token positions; pairwise subspace overlap.

**Linear decodability:** Cross-validated $R^2$ for source weights and hidden beliefs
for all three sources.

**Nonlinear vs. linear decodability of source identity:** Train a k-nearest-neighbor
classifier (k=5) and a linear classifier on final-position residual stream
activations. The gap between KNN and linear accuracy is the key diagnostic — a
large gap confirms categorical encoding; convergence of the two suggests the model
has shifted to a linear encoding of source identity consistent with the FWH.

---

### 4.4 What This Would Tell Us

**When does the FWH succeed?** Source identity is only worth encoding as a separate
factor if sources are statistically distinguishable. This experiment maps out the
distinguishability threshold below which the FWH prediction breaks down — directly
connecting the theory's requirements to an empirically measurable parameter.

**How does source encoding transition from categorical to linear?** The Task 3
finding may be a consequence of insufficient distinguishability rather than a
fundamental property of non-ergodic structure. If at high $\lambda$ the
linear-nonlinear gap closes, it would suggest transformers encode source identity
linearly when evidence is sufficient and fall back to categorical encoding when it
is not — a new finding about how transformers handle uncertain hierarchical
inference that goes beyond what the paper studied.
